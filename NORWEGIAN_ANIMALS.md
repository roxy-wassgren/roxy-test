# Norske dyr

## Elg (Moose)
*   Norway has an estimated 100,000 to 120,000 moose, with the highest densities in central and eastern Norway.
*   About 30,000 moose are harvested annually during the autumn hunt, making it one of Norway's most important game species.
*   Bulls can weigh up to 600 kg, while cows typically weigh around 350 kg.
*   Moose cause around 1,500 traffic accidents and over 1,000 train collisions each year in Norway.
*   They primarily feed on birch, willow, and pine, and can consume up to 50 kg of food per day in summer.

## Brunbjorn (Brown Bear)
*   Norway has a small brown bear population of around 150 to 180 individuals, mostly along the border with Sweden, Finland, and Russia.
*   The population was nearly extinct in the early 1900s due to hunting but has recovered slowly thanks to legal protection since 1973.
*   Norwegian brown bears are generally shy and avoid human contact.
*   They hibernate from October to April in dens dug into hillsides or under large boulders.
*   Bears in Norway feed heavily on berries, ants, and moose calves in spring.

## Jerv (Wolverine)
*   Norway has approximately 350 to 400 wolverines, mainly in alpine and northern regions.
*   The wolverine is classified as Endangered (EN) in Norway's Red List.
*   They are notorious for preying on semi-domesticated reindeer and sheep, causing significant conflicts with herders and farmers.
*   Wolverines are remarkably strong for their size, capable of dragging prey several times their own weight across snow.
*   Females give birth in snow dens at high altitude in late February to March.

## Havorn (White-tailed Eagle)
*   Norway holds the largest population of white-tailed eagles in Europe, with over 3,500 breeding pairs.
*   They are found mainly along the Norwegian coast, from Rogaland in the south to Finnmark in the north.
*   With a wingspan of up to 2.45 meters, they are the largest bird of prey in Northern Europe.
*   The population was nearly wiped out by the 1970s due to pesticides and persecution but has made a strong recovery.
*   They feed primarily on fish, seabirds, and carrion.

## Villrein (Wild Reindeer)
*   Norway is the only country in Europe with wild (non-domesticated) mountain reindeer, with about 25,000 individuals.
*   Wild reindeer are found in the mountain plateaus of southern Norway, including Hardangervidda, the largest herd area.
*   They have been present in Norway since the last ice age, over 10,000 years ago.
*   Chronic wasting disease (CWD) was detected in wild reindeer in 2016, leading to the controversial culling of the entire Nordfjella herd.
*   Habitat fragmentation from roads, cabins, and tourism is considered the greatest long-term threat.

## Gaupe (Eurasian Lynx)
*   Norway has around 300 to 350 lynx, managed through licensed hunting to control their impact on livestock.
*   The Eurasian lynx is the largest wild cat in Europe and is classified as Endangered (EN) in Norway.
*   They are solitary and elusive, primarily hunting roe deer, hares, and sometimes sheep.
*   Lynx are found across most of Norway but are most common in forested regions of central and southern Norway.
*   A single lynx may patrol a territory of 300 to 1,000 square kilometers.
